    • Albums of the decade 2010 -2019

    Another decade has passed and the future has officially arrived – 2020 occurs one year later than the period of original Blade Runner’s world. In terms of music, 2010-2019 was one of the best decades. In terms of private life, it was one of the most beautiful and the hardest periods in which I experienced the greatest love and got through the greatest of pains.

    The decade began in a slightly sad and melancholic tone. I listened to Massive Attack’s Heligoland during the winter 2010 while receiving an antibiotic therapy – and this was the overall feel of that year: cold, sad and lonely. The next two years were alright, but nothing seemed to be as it should. As I remember, there was that winter feel all over. I guess listening to Kate Bush’s 50 Words For Snow helped maintain that feeling.

    The Black Keys where a leftover from a previous life, while Tame Impala lead me into modern psychedelic rock. I listened to Melody’s Echo Chamber while learning French and Kurt Vile’s Walking On A Pretty Daze took me to sunny places.

    I met the love of my life on Zdravo Mladi Festival. We talked about The Soft Moon’s performance at the festival and stayed together for the whole night (and we’ve been together ever since). In 2015, he played me Birds of passage and her nocturnal music took the form of the pain that I carried inside me. In 2016, we saw Föllakzoid and Moon Duo playing at the same festival. The following year, there was Slowdive. In the recent years, the festival’s programme began to cater the tastes in music embraced by younger generations, so we lost interest.

    2017 was definitely the year of King Gizzard and the Lizard Wizard and the sweet discovery of Australia’s music scene. 2018 was the year of psychedelic (guitar) music coming from all corners of the world: Wooden Shjips, The Babe Rainbow, Kikagaku Mojo, Minami Deutsch. The re-listening to Dead can Dance’s catalog represented a rediscovery of a new, long forgotten world.

    2019 was an year of calmness and introspection, setting life priorities straight and greater restriction of the things that I let occupy my time and mental space. Tranquility and harmony, backed up by ambient music that was played by, well, you know who. The event of the year was the Dead can Dance’s concert in Budapest. Last minute tickets secured us places in the last row of a gargantuan sports arena. Yet, all of that was insignificant in comparison to the beauty of the sonic spectacle provided by Lisa and Brendan who marked their thirty-ninth anniversary of the band’s existence.

    About

    In the lost and found is a joint project of Elena Davcheva and Igor Stojanovski. The website is a public archive of our own writings about music as well as a platform for promoting new and exciting bands and artists.

    We are mostly focused on independent and underground music. More specifically, we are into psychedelic rock and its derivatives, different forms of ambient and mainly experimental music. Yet, we do not shy away from more exposed music artists as well. No rules apply here and we are not trying to be snobs at all.
