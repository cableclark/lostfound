    • Past, Present and Yet To Come: The music of 2018

    It is amazing how easily I got used to the new year and 2018 already seams so far away. Throughout December I was thinking back to the year that passed by, but I got the festive blues and couldn’t find the strength to summarize it. On Christmas Eve I gathered my thoughts and, just like in the Christmas Carol, I let the Ghosts of the Past, the Present and the Yet To Come to remind me on what was before, how is it now and what the future might be.

    Past

    I spent large portion of 2018 with the music of Kate Bush. I cannot even explain myself what kind of a force is it that drags me towards her, but her music simply enchants me. Hearing her saying „Take my shoes off/ and throw them in the lake“, I literally feel the urge to throw my shoes and simply start walking wherever my feet may take me. I mostly listened to The Sensual World, Hounds of Love and Aerial.

    Smashing Pumpkins were a big deal for me back in the ‘90s, spending hours and hours listening to Mellon Collie and the Infinite Sadness, Siamese Dream and Pisces Iscariot. Yet, their debut album Gish remained quite unfamiliar, so this year I went back to correct that.

    Gish has the unfortunate destiny to be constantly compared, and thus overshadowed, by Nirvana’s Nevermind, both produced by Butch Vig (Garbage) and released the same year of 1991. Unlike Nirvana, Smashing Pumpkins did not gain much of a fame and fortune at that time, but merely appeared on the scene and announced their presence. However, the following years they took over the alternative rock music scene and marked the music of the ‘90s. This year The Smashing Pumpkins made a come-back with a new album and a tour named “Shiny And Oh So Bright” which I gladly awaited. But, I regret to state that these Smashing Pumpkins are some faded away version of that once cool band from the ‘90s. They had it, they lost it.

    This year marks 20 years from the releasing of Air’s debut album Moon Safari. All these years I’ve never stopped listening to this album and I’ve never ever got bored of it. Being unpretentious and modest, it is a unique masterpiece combining electronic music, jazz, synth-pop, space-rock and trip-hop with a French twist.

    Reading the memoir “Drugs are nice” by Lisa Crystal Carver (Lisa Suckdog), a former partner of Bill Callahan, led me to re-listen his 2011 album Apocalypse. I love it how the sound on this album is so crisp and clear that I have the feeling I can almost touch it.

    From the memoir one could get the picture for the most obscure underground scene of the ‘80s and early ‘90s, various experimental and noise rock musicians, performance artists and complete lunatics like GG Allin, which were Lisa’s friends. I couldn’t understand how could the eccentric Lisa and the quiet Bill ever get along. She wanted to become famous, and Bill didn’t have much of ambitions. From today’s perspective, they both achieved the totally opposite.

    Present

    During January we had the time to listen to King Gizzard and the Lizard Wizard’s Gumboot Soup which came out at the very end of 2017. The alternative Australian scene was a sweet discovery, but the music coming from Japan was even more exotic. The psychedelic and kraut rock music of Japanese bands Kikagaku Moyo and Minami Deutch and their 2018 albums Masana Temples and With Dim Light respectively were one of the most listened throughout 2018.

    The summer was marked by psychedelic rock listening to Wooden Shjips, Double Rainbow and Triptides. We saw Wooden Shjips at Zdravo Mladi Festival in September and the good old San-Fran hippies took us on a ride to the psychedelic landscapes of their music. Their melodies are crazy enough to be fun, but carefully dosed not to be heavy to digest.

    At the beginning of December Dead Can Dance released their latest album Dionysus, which consists of seven songs divided in two parts named Act I and Act II and it covers different aspects of the myth of the ancient god Dionysus. The music on the album has this specific Balkan melody while the band was using instruments from all over the world.

    There is something primordial in the music of Dead Can Dance which I recognize as something that belongs to me, like some long forgotten part of myself. I immediately fell in love with this album.

    Yet To Come

    I wrongfully ignored the Ghost of the Yet To Come and didn’t assume that he could bring me another loss of a loved one at the very beginning of the year. For better or worse, once faced with a great loss in life, one learns how to deal with future pains of such scale. It doesn’t get easier, but you nevertheless learn.

    I want to believe in the philosophy of the Tralfamadorians from Kurt Vonnegut’s Slaughterhouse-Five who perceive time as a whole and the destiny of every human as an eternal present. According to them, when someone dies, it just seems so, because one is still alive in the past.

    I like that idea. It gives me comfort and soothes me.

    About

    In the lost and found is a joint project of Elena Davcheva and Igor Stojanovski. The website is a public archive of our own writings about music as well as a platform for promoting new and exciting bands and artists.

    We are mostly focused on independent and underground music. More specifically, we are into psychedelic rock and its derivatives, different forms of ambient and mainly experimental music. Yet, we do not shy away from more exposed music artists as well. No rules apply here and we are not trying to be snobs at all.
